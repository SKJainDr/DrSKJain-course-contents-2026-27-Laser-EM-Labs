Unit 1 — Laser Fundamentals & Population Inversion

Spontaneous vs stimulated emission. Spontaneous emission occurs randomly and independently for each excited atom, emitting incoherent light in random directions. Stimulated emission is triggered by an incident photon of matching energy and produces a second photon that is a coherent copy — identical phase, frequency, polarization and direction — of the incident one. Laser gain relies entirely on stimulated emission dominating over spontaneous emission and absorption.

Einstein's A and B coefficients. For two levels 1 (lower) and 2 (upper): spontaneous emission rate = A₂₁N₂; stimulated emission rate = B₂₁N₂ρ(ν); absorption rate = B₁₂N₁ρ(ν), where ρ(ν) is the radiation energy density. Detailed balance in thermal equilibrium gives the relations B₁₂ = B₂₁ and A₂₁/B₂₁ = 8πhν³/c³ — showing that at optical frequencies spontaneous emission is normally overwhelming unless population inversion is engineered.

Population inversion (N₂ > N₁) cannot be achieved in a simple two-level system by any amount of pumping (it saturates at N₁=N₂). Practical lasers use three-level (e.g. Ruby: pump to a short-lived band, fast non-radiative decay to a metastable level, lasing transition to the ground state — inherently inefficient, pulsed) or four-level schemes (e.g. Nd:YAG, He-Ne: lasing transition ends on a level above the ground state that empties quickly, making inversion much easier to sustain — enabling continuous-wave operation).

Optical cavity / resonator (two mirrors, one partially transmitting) provides optical feedback, selecting specific longitudinal/transverse modes and building up coherent intensity. Coherence: temporal coherence relates to the laser's narrow linewidth (long coherence length); spatial coherence relates to the uniform phase across the beam's wavefront, giving low divergence and tight focusability.

Unit 2 — Laser Types

Solid-state lasers use ions doped in a crystalline or glass host. Ruby laser (Cr³⁺ in Al₂O₃, 694.3 nm, three-level, optically pumped by a flashlamp, typically pulsed). Nd:YAG (Nd³⁺ in Y₃Al₅O₁₂, 1064 nm, four-level, can run CW or pulsed, widely used in industrial cutting/welding and medicine).

Gas lasers. He-Ne laser (632.8 nm visible red, four-level; He atoms are excited by electric discharge and transfer energy to Ne via near-resonant collisions, producing population inversion in Ne). Ionic lasers (e.g. Argon-ion) use ionized gas for higher-energy visible/UV transitions. Molecular lasers (e.g. CO₂ laser, 10.6 μm) exploit vibrational-rotational transitions and deliver very high output powers, dominant in industrial cutting/welding.

Excimer lasers use short-lived diatomic molecules that exist only in the excited state (e.g. ArF, KrF), emitting in the UV — central to photolithography and eye surgery (LASIK).

Liquid (dye) lasers use organic dye molecules in solution; their broad vibrational bands make them widely tunable across the visible spectrum.

Semiconductor (diode) lasers use a forward-biased p-n junction where electron-hole recombination produces light; compact, efficient, directly current-modulated — the workhorse of telecom and consumer optics. Fibre lasers use a rare-earth-doped optical fibre as the gain medium, offering excellent beam quality and efficient heat dissipation for high-power industrial and telecom applications.

Unit 3 — Holography & Fibre Optics

Holography records the complete wavefront (both amplitude and phase) scattered from an object by interfering it with a coherent reference beam on a high-resolution photographic emulsion. Unlike an ordinary photograph (intensity only), a hologram reconstructs a full 3-D image when illuminated by a beam matching the original reference beam. HNDT (Holographic Non-Destructive Testing) compares two holograms of a component recorded before and after stress, revealing microscopic surface deformation via interference fringes — used for crack/defect detection without damaging the part. Holographic data storage exploits the volume of the recording medium to store many holograms in the same physical space via angular or wavelength multiplexing, offering very high data density.

Optical fibre communication. Light is guided along the fibre core by total internal reflection. Step-index vs graded-index and single-mode vs multimode fibre designs trade off bandwidth, dispersion and ease of coupling. Fibre amplifiers (e.g. Erbium-Doped Fibre Amplifiers, EDFA) amplify optical signals directly, without opto-electronic conversion, and are essential to long-haul telecom links. Fibre-optic sensors exploit changes in intensity, phase or polarization of guided light to sense strain, temperature, pressure or chemical concentration remotely and immune to electromagnetic interference.

Unit 4 — Lasers in Science

Saturation spectroscopy is a Doppler-free technique: a strong "pump" beam saturates (partially depletes) a velocity subgroup of atoms resonant with it, and a weaker counter-propagating "probe" beam reveals a narrow dip (Lamb dip) at line centre, resolving structure hidden under Doppler broadening.

Excited-state spectroscopy uses lasers to selectively populate excited states and probe their subsequent decay, revealing lifetimes and branching ratios inaccessible with conventional lamps.

Non-linear spectroscopy exploits high laser intensities to drive multi-photon and non-linear optical processes (e.g. two-photon absorption, harmonic generation), extending accessible transitions and improving resolution.

Time-domain spectroscopy uses ultrafast (pico/femtosecond) laser pulses combined with pump-probe techniques to follow chemical reaction dynamics and carrier relaxation in materials in real time.

Stimulated Raman emission converts pump photons into a Stokes-shifted output through stimulated inelastic scattering, used for tunable coherent light generation and Raman lasers/amplifiers. Laser fusion (Inertial Confinement Fusion) uses many high-power laser beams focused symmetrically on a fuel pellet to compress and heat it to fusion conditions. Medical applications include laser surgery (cutting/coagulation via controlled tissue ablation), ophthalmology (LASIK, retinal photocoagulation), and dermatology; photo-chemical applications use laser light to selectively drive chemical reactions (photodynamic therapy, laser-induced chemistry).

Unit 5 — Lasers in Industry

Materials processing exploits the laser's ability to deliver highly localized, controllable energy. Drilling and cutting use focused pulsed or CW beams to vaporize/melt material along a precise path, with minimal heat-affected zone compared to mechanical methods. Welding uses a focused beam to melt and fuse materials at the joint, achieving deep, narrow welds with low distortion. Alloying uses a laser to melt a thin surface layer together with an added alloying element to modify surface composition and properties locally.

Thermal deposition & Laser Chemical Vapour Deposition (LCVD) use laser energy to decompose precursor gases and deposit thin films or 3-D microstructures at precisely controlled locations — used in microelectronics and rapid prototyping.

Laser hardening rapidly heats a thin surface layer of a metal component (e.g. gear teeth, cam surfaces) followed by rapid self-quenching (heat conducted away by the cold bulk material) to form a hard martensitic layer, improving wear resistance without distorting the whole part. Laser annealing uses controlled heating to relieve internal stresses or activate dopants (as in semiconductor processing) without melting the bulk material.

LIDAR (Light Detection and Ranging) measures distance/velocity by timing the round-trip of laser pulses reflected from a target, with applications in atmospheric sensing, autonomous vehicles, topographic mapping and meteorology.

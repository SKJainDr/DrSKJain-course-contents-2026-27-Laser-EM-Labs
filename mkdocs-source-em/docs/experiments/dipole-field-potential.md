## Aim

To compute and plot the electric field and potential of an electric dipole along its axial and equatorial lines, and to study the angular variation of the field at a fixed distance.

## Theoretical Background

An electric dipole consists of two equal and opposite charges +q and −q separated by a small distance 2a, with dipole moment p = q·(2a) directed from −q to +q. For distances r ≫ a, the potential at a point making angle θ with the dipole axis is V = p·cosθ/(4πε₀r²), and the field has a radial component E_r = 2p·cosθ/(4πε₀r³) and a tangential component E_θ = p·sinθ/(4πε₀r³). On the axial line (θ = 0) the field is E_axial = 2kp/r³, twice the equatorial-line field E_equatorial = kp/r³ (θ = 90°), and the two fields are oppositely directed relative to p. This 1/r³ falloff (steeper than the 1/r² of a point charge) is a signature of the dipole and is the basis for understanding polar-molecule interactions and dielectric polarization.

## Governing Formula

$$ V = p·cosθ / (4πε₀ r²),  E_axial = 2kp/r³,  E_equatorial = kp/r³ $$

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| p | dipole moment, p = q·2a (C·m) |
| r | distance from the dipole centre (m) |
| θ | angle from the dipole axis |
| k | 1/4πε₀ = 8.988×10⁹ N·m²/C² |

## Procedure

1. Set the probe on the axial line (θ = 0°) and record E and V as r is increased from 5 cm to 50 cm.
2. Plot log E versus log r for the axial case and verify the slope is −3 (not −2 as for a single point charge).
3. Move the probe to the equatorial line (θ = 90°) and confirm V ≈ 0 there at every r.
4. At a fixed r = 20 cm, sweep θ from 0° to 180° and tabulate E and V; note the sign reversal of V past θ = 90°.
5. Compare the axial field magnitude with the equatorial field magnitude at the same r; verify the 2:1 ratio.
6. Increase the separation 2a and confirm the point-dipole formulas remain accurate only while r ≫ a.

## Precautions

- Always keep r ≫ 2a so the point-dipole approximation is valid; note the deviation if this is violated.
- Track the sign of the potential carefully — it is positive on the +q side and negative on the −q side of the equatorial plane.
- When sweeping θ, keep r fixed to isolate the angular dependence cleanly.
- Distinguish clearly between the radial and tangential components of E when off-axis.

## Maximum Permissible Error

$$ ΔE/E = Δq/q + Δ(2a)/(2a) + 3Δr/r $$

E_axial = 2kp/r³ with p=q·(2a) varies as r⁻³, so its fractional uncertainty carries a factor of 3 from r.

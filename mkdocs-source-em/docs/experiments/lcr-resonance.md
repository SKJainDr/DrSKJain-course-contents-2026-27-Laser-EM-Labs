## Aim

To study the resonance behaviour of a series LCR circuit driven by an AC source, locate the resonant frequency, and determine the quality factor from the sharpness of the resonance curve.

## Theoretical Background

In a series LCR circuit driven by a sinusoidal source of angular frequency ω, the impedance is Z = √[R² + (ωL − 1/ωC)²]. The current amplitude I(ω) = V₀/Z is maximum when the reactive term vanishes, at the resonant angular frequency ω₀ = 1/√(LC), where the impedance reduces to purely R and the circuit behaves resistively with the largest possible current. The sharpness of the resonance peak is characterised by the quality factor Q = ω₀L/R = (1/R)√(L/C) = ω₀/Δω, where Δω is the full width of the resonance curve at half the maximum power (equivalently, at I = I_max/√2). A higher Q means a lower R relative to √(L/C), giving a narrower, taller resonance peak and better frequency selectivity, as exploited in radio tuning circuits.

## Governing Formula

$$ ω₀ = 1/√(LC),   Q = ω₀L/R = (1/R)√(L/C) $$

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| ω₀ | resonant angular frequency (rad/s) |
| L | inductance (H) |
| C | capacitance (F) |
| R | resistance (Ω) |
| Q | quality factor (dimensionless) |

## Procedure

1. Sweep the drive frequency f across a wide range and plot the current amplitude I(f); locate the peak and compare with f₀ = 1/(2π√LC).
2. Read off the half-power frequencies (where I = I_max/√2) and compute the bandwidth Δf; hence determine Q = f₀/Δf.
3. Increase R (keeping L, C fixed) and observe the resonance peak becoming shorter and broader; verify Q decreases as expected.
4. Vary L and C while keeping their product LC fixed (so f₀ is unchanged) and observe how the peak height and width change with √(L/C).
5. At resonance, verify that the voltage across L and across C individually can each exceed the source voltage V₀, while remaining 180° out of phase with each other.
6. Compute Q by two independent methods — from the bandwidth, and from Q = ω₀L/R — and compare.

## Precautions

- Sweep frequency finely (small steps) near resonance, since the peak can be narrow for high-Q circuits.
- Keep the source amplitude V₀ fixed throughout a frequency sweep.
- Vary only one of R, L, C at a time when studying their individual effects on the resonance curve.
- Remember the individual voltages across L and C can exceed the source voltage at resonance — this is expected, not an error.

## Maximum Permissible Error

$$ Δf₀/f₀ = ½(ΔL/L + ΔC/C) $$

f₀ = 1/(2π√LC) involves a square root of the product LC, halving the summed fractional errors.

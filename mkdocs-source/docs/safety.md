# Laser Safety, Precautions & Potential Harm

!!! warning "Essential Reading Before Any Real Laser Experiment"
    Every experiment in this lab that involves a real laser (Newton's rings/biprism with laser sources, He-Ne alignment, Ruby/Nd:YAG demonstrations, laser diffraction) carries real hazards. Read this section before touching any laser apparatus.

!!! danger "Warning"
    Never look directly into a laser beam or its specular reflection — even a low-power laser pointer can cause permanent retinal damage before your blink reflex can protect you.

## Laser Hazard Classes (IEC 60825-1)

| Class | Typical power | Hazard level | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| Class 1 | < 0.4 mW (visible, CW) | Safe under normal use; no known biological hazard | Laser printers, CD/DVD/Blu-ray players (enclosed) |
| Class 1M | Varies | Safe unless viewed with magnifying optics (binoculars, telescopes) | Fibre-communication test sources |
| Class 2 | < 1 mW (visible) | Blink reflex (~0.25 s) normally protects the eye — do not stare deliberately | Basic laser pointers, barcode scanners |
| Class 3R | 1 – 5 mW | Low risk but direct beam viewing should be avoided | Some laser pointers, alignment lasers |
| Class 3B | 5 – 500 mW | Hazardous to the eye from direct beam and specular reflections; diffuse reflection usually safe | Typical teaching He-Ne and diode lasers used in these experiments |
| Class 4 | > 500 mW | Hazardous to eye and skin from direct, specular AND diffuse reflections; fire and ignition risk | Nd:YAG/Ruby/CO2 industrial and research lasers (processing workshops) |

## Potential Harm

### Eye Injury

The eye focuses laser light onto the retina exactly as it focuses any other light, but a coherent, collimated beam concentrates enormous power density onto a tiny retinal spot. Possible outcomes include permanent blind spots, retinal haemorrhage, and (for UV/IR lasers) corneal or lens damage. Damage from a visible Class 3B/4 beam can occur faster than the blink reflex.

### Skin & Tissue Burns

Class 4 beams (and some focused Class 3B beams) can burn skin, ignite clothing, paper, or solvent vapours, and damage optical components. Reflected beams from jewellery, watches or metallic tools are a commonly overlooked hazard.

### Electrical Hazards

Gas lasers (He-Ne, Ar-ion) and flashlamp-pumped solid-state lasers (Ruby) use high-voltage power supplies. Capacitor banks in pulsed systems can retain a lethal charge even after the supply is switched off.

### Chemical / Fume Hazards

Dye lasers use organic dyes in solvents that may be toxic, flammable or carcinogenic; some excimer lasers use toxic or corrosive gases that require proper handling and ventilation.

## Mandatory Precautions Before & During a Laser Experiment

- Wear correctly rated laser safety goggles matched to the laser's wavelength and power, not generic sunglasses.
- Never place your eye at beam height; keep the beam path at chest level or below whenever possible.
- Remove reflective objects (wristwatches, rings, metallic pens, ID cards) from the beam path before aligning optics.
- Enclose or terminate the beam path with a non-reflective beam block/dump.
- Work in a well-lit room when practical, since dark rooms dilate the pupil and increase exposure severity.
- Never look into the beam to 'check if it's on' — use a beam card, IR viewer, or power meter instead.
- Align at reduced power first, increasing to working power only once the beam path is fully controlled.
- Post a warning sign and, for Class 3B/4 systems, use an interlocked door or curtain.
- Know your laser's class and maximum permissible exposure (MPE) before starting.
- Never defeat safety interlocks or attempt to service internal high-voltage circuitry without qualified supervision.
- Keep flammable solvents, papers, and gas cylinders away from the direct beam path of any Class 4 laser.
- Report every incident, even a suspected brief exposure, to the lab in-charge immediately.

## Common Mistakes That Lead to Injury

- Looking along the beam axis 'to see if it's aligned' instead of using a card or viewer.
- Assuming a 'low-power' laser pointer or diode module is harmless.
- Leaning over the optical table so the eye is at beam height while adjusting mirrors or lenses.
- Wearing sunglasses instead of laser-rated goggles.
- Not accounting for specular reflections from lens mounts, mirror edges, or metal tools left on the table.
- Running a Class 3B/4 beam through an open door or window into a corridor or another room.

!!! note "Bottom line"
    The coherence and directionality that make laser light so scientifically useful are exactly the properties that make it uniquely dangerous to eyes and skin compared with ordinary light of the same total power. Respect every laser as a genuine hazard, regardless of its apparent brightness to the naked eye.

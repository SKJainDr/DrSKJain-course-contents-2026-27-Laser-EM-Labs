Wave Optics · Module 4

Dispersion by a Prism

A prism bends different wavelengths by different amounts because refractive index depends on wavelength (normal dispersion: n is higher for blue/violet than for red). Adjust the prism's dispersive power and watch white light spread into a spectrum.

📖 Full theory, formulas & procedure for this in the User Guide → 📝 Open Lab Record & Viva-Voce Booklet (PDF) →
Cauchy's relation n(λ) = A + B/λ² captures normal dispersion: refractive index (and hence the amount of bending) increases as wavelength decreases, so violet bends more than red — this is why a prism, unlike a mirror, separates white light into a rainbow. At steep angles of incidence the internal angle at the second face can exceed the critical angle for the higher-index (violet/blue) end of the spectrum before it does for red — those wavelengths then undergo total internal reflection (TIR) instead of emerging, shown here for visualization only (not something you need to record).
Wave Optics · Interferometry

🪞 Michelson Interferometer

A beamsplitter divides a laser beam into two arms, each ending in a mirror. Moving mirror M₁ changes the path difference between the arms, and the recombined beams form a circular fringe pattern that expands or collapses at the centre as the mirror moves — the classic length-measurement instrument.

🌌
Why this matters: This exact interferometer design, scaled up to 4-km arms, is how LIGO detected gravitational waves in 2015 — measuring a mirror displacement 10,000× smaller than a proton. The same principle runs everyday precision length gauges and laser-interferometric position sensors in CNC machine tools.

Each time Δm increases by exactly 1, one ring has collapsed into (or emerged from) the centre of the pattern — so counting fringes while moving M₁ a known number of times lets you measure displacement to a fraction of a wavelength. This is the operating principle behind the original Michelson–Morley experiment (BAS101 Unit 1) and modern laser interferometric length/vibration sensors.

Particle Nature of Light · Experiment 1

The Photoelectric Effect

Shine light of a chosen frequency and intensity onto a metal plate. Below the threshold frequency, no electrons are ejected — however bright the light. Above threshold, electrons are ejected instantly, with a maximum kinetic energy that depends only on frequency, not intensity.

💡
Why this matters: This single experiment is why Einstein won the 1921 Nobel Prize — it proved light is quantized. It's also the working principle behind every photodiode, solar cell, camera image sensor and photomultiplier tube you use today.
📖 Full theory, formulas & procedure for this in the User Guide → 📝 Open Lab Record & Viva-Voce Booklet (PDF) →
Photon energy E = hν = eV
Max KE of electron = E − φ = eV
Stopping voltage V₀ = V
Key insight: raising intensity increases the number of ejected electrons (more current) but never their maximum kinetic energy — only raising the frequency above threshold does that. This single fact could not be explained by classical wave theory and was one of Einstein's central pieces of evidence for light quanta (photons).
Particle Nature of Light · Experiment 2

Compton Scattering

An X-ray photon collides with a free electron like a billiard-ball collision, transferring some momentum and losing energy — its wavelength increases. Change the scattering angle and see the wavelength shift predicted by the Compton formula, direct evidence that photons carry momentum p = h/λ.

🏥
Why this matters: Compton scattering is the dominant way medium-energy X-rays and gamma rays lose energy in tissue — so it directly governs radiation dose calculations in CT scans and radiotherapy, and is the detection principle behind Compton-camera gamma-ray telescopes used in astronomy.
📖 Full theory, formulas & procedure for this in the User Guide → 📝 Open Lab Record & Viva-Voce Booklet (PDF) →
Compton shift Δλ = (h/mec)(1−cosθ) = pm
Scattered wavelength λ' = pm
Δλ = λC(1 − cosθ), where λC = h/(mec) = 2.426 pm (Compton wavelength)
The wavelength shift depends only on the scattering angle, never on the target material or incident intensity — a signature that only makes sense if the photon behaves as a discrete particle with definite energy hν and momentum h/λ, colliding elastically with the electron.
Particle Nature of Light · Experiment 3

Single-Photon Double-Slit Build-up

Photons are sent through a double slit one at a time. Each one lands as a single, random-looking dot — yet after thousands of individual "particle" arrivals, an interference pattern emerges. This is perhaps the single most direct demonstration of wave–particle duality.

🔐
Why this matters: This is the same single-photon behaviour that underlies quantum key distribution and quantum computing — technologies that rely on individual photons being genuinely indivisible, unpredictable quanta rather than tiny classical particles.
📖 Full theory, formulas & procedure for this in the User Guide → 📝 Open Lab Record & Viva-Voce Booklet (PDF) →
Photons detected: 0
Each dot marks one detected photon at a random position, weighted by the same probability distribution (∝ cos²) that classical wave theory predicts for double-slit intensity. No single photon "knows" about the emerging pattern — the pattern only appears statistically, over many independent detection events.

Monochromaticity & Spectral Purity

Monochromaticity describes how close a source comes to emitting a single wavelength. Compare the emission spectrum of a laser (extremely narrow line) against an LED and a white-light source (broad continuum).

Essential Reading Before Any Real Laser Experiment

Laser Safety, Precautions & Potential Harm

Every experiment in BAS101, MPY301, BAS151 and MPY351 that involves a real laser (Newton's rings/biprism with laser sources, He-Ne alignment, Ruby/Nd:YAG demonstrations, laser diffraction) carries real hazards. This section is not optional reading — review it before touching any laser apparatus in the lab.

📖 Full theory, formulas & procedure for this in the User Guide →
⚠️ Never look directly into a laser beam or its specular reflection — even a low-power laser pointer can cause permanent retinal damage before your blink reflex can protect you.

Laser Hazard Classes (IEC 60825-1)

ClassTypical powerHazard levelExamples
Class 1< 0.4 mW (visible, CW)Safe under normal use; no known biological hazardLaser printers, CD/DVD/Blu-ray players (enclosed)
Class 1MVariesSafe unless viewed with magnifying optics (binoculars, telescopes)Fibre-communication test sources
Class 2< 1 mW (visible)Blink reflex (~0.25 s) normally protects the eye — do not stare deliberatelyBasic laser pointers, barcode scanners
Class 3R1–5 mWLow risk but direct beam viewing should be avoidedSome laser pointers, alignment lasers
Class 3B5–500 mWHazardous to the eye from direct beam and specular reflections; diffuse reflection usually safeTypical teaching He-Ne and diode lasers used in BAS101/MPY301/BAS151/MPY351 experiments
Class 4> 500 mWHazardous to eye and skin from direct, specular AND diffuse reflections; fire and ignition riskNd:YAG/Ruby/CO₂ industrial and research lasers (MPY301 Unit 5 processing lasers)
Most laser sets used in undergraduate/postgraduate optics labs (low-power He-Ne, laser diode modules) are Class 2 or 3R/3B — still capable of permanent eye damage from direct or reflected beam exposure, so full precautions apply regardless of the modest power involved.

👁️ Eye Injury

The eye focuses laser light onto the retina exactly as it focuses any other light — but a coherent, collimated beam concentrates enormous power density onto a tiny retinal spot. Possible outcomes include permanent blind spots (retinal burns/scotomas), retinal haemorrhage, and — for UV/IR lasers — corneal or lens damage, since these wavelengths are absorbed before reaching the retina at all. Damage from a visible Class 3B/4 beam can occur in a fraction of a second, faster than the natural blink reflex.

🔥 Skin & Tissue Burns

Class 4 beams (and even some Class 3B beams focused to a point) can burn skin, ignite clothing, paper, or solvent vapours, and damage optical components not rated for the beam's power. Reflected beams from jewellery, watches or metallic tools are a commonly overlooked hazard.

⚡ Electrical Hazards

Gas lasers (He-Ne, Ar-ion) and flashlamp-pumped solid-state lasers (Ruby) use high-voltage power supplies (kilovolts) to strike the discharge or fire the flashlamp. Capacitor banks in pulsed systems can retain a lethal charge even after the supply is switched off.

🧪 Chemical / Fume Hazards

Dye lasers use organic dyes dissolved in solvents that may be toxic, flammable or carcinogenic; some excimer lasers use toxic or corrosive gases (e.g. fluorine-based mixtures) that require proper gas handling and ventilation.

✅ Mandatory Precautions Before & During a Laser Experiment

  • Wear correctly rated laser safety goggles — matched to the laser's wavelength and power, not generic sunglasses or safety glasses. Verify the optical density (OD) rating covers your specific laser.
  • Never place your eye at beam height. Keep the beam path at chest level or below whenever possible, and always know exactly where the beam and its reflections are travelling.
  • Remove reflective objects — wristwatches, rings, metallic pens, ID cards — from the beam path before aligning any optics.
  • Enclose or terminate the beam path with a non-reflective beam block/dump at the end of its travel so it cannot escape into occupied space.
  • Work in a well-lit room when practical — dark rooms encourage the pupil to dilate, increasing the amount of light entering the eye if an accidental exposure occurs. (Some alignment tasks genuinely require dim light — in that case, keep exposure time and direct viewing to an absolute minimum and rely on beam viewing cards, not the naked eye.)
  • Never look into the beam to "check if it's on". Use a beam card, IR viewer, or power meter instead — never your eye, and never a co-worker's eye.
  • Align at reduced power first wherever the laser/power supply allows, then increase to working power only once the beam path is fully controlled and enclosed.
  • Post a warning sign ("LASER IN USE — DO NOT ENTER") and, for Class 3B/4 systems, use an interlocked door or curtain so nobody can walk into the beam path unexpectedly.
  • Know your laser's class and maximum permissible exposure (MPE) before starting; this information is on the manufacturer's label and safety datasheet.
  • Never defeat safety interlocks on laser housings, and never attempt to service the internal high-voltage power supply or flashlamp circuitry without qualified supervision — stored charge can be lethal even with the unit "off".
  • Keep flammable solvents, papers, and gas cylinders away from the direct beam path of any Class 4 laser used for materials-processing demonstrations.
  • Report every incident — even a suspected brief exposure — to the lab in-charge immediately; retinal injury is not always immediately painful or obvious.

🚫 Common Mistakes That Lead to Injury

  • Looking along the beam axis "to see if it's aligned" instead of using a card or viewer.
  • Assuming a "low-power" laser pointer or diode module is harmless — many exceed Class 2 limits and can still injure the retina with sustained or repeated exposure.
  • Leaning over the optical table so the eye is at beam height while adjusting mirrors or lenses.
  • Wearing sunglasses instead of laser-rated goggles — sunglasses provide essentially zero protection against the specific, narrow-band wavelength a laser emits.
  • Not accounting for specular reflections from lens mounts, mirror edges, or metal tools left on the table.
  • Running a Class 3B/4 beam through an open door or window into a corridor or another room.
Bottom line: the coherence and directionality that make laser light so scientifically useful (Sections "Coherence" and "Directionality" in this lab) are exactly the properties that make it uniquely dangerous to eyes and skin compared with ordinary light of the same total power. Respect every laser as a genuine hazard, regardless of its apparent brightness to the naked eye.
